// split decision

Push / Pull / Legs CHOSEN

At 3 days/week with a goal of full-body aesthetics, PPL wins. Each session has a clear mechanical theme — pressing, pulling, or leg drive — which makes progressive overload tracking clean and simple. You never hit the same movement pattern on back-to-back days, so recovery is built in. Core is woven into every session naturally.

Upper / Lower SKIP

Upper/Lower is excellent at 4 days/week. At 3 days you'd be repeating the same muscles twice in one week but with less frequency per movement. Push/Pull/Lower gives you cleaner volume distribution and better compound focus at your current frequency.

PUSH DAY
Chest · Shoulders · Triceps · Core (anti-extension)

Push day drives the chest, anterior deltoid, and triceps. Lead with a heavy horizontal press when you're fresh, then follow with an incline variation, shoulder isolation, and tricep work. Core finisher uses anti-extension patterns — the most functional six-pack builder. Rest 2–3 min on compounds, 60–90 sec on isolation.

Chest — primary
Barbell Bench Press
Barbell
4 × 6–8 ▾
Technique
Retract and depress scapulae before unracking. Slight arch, feet planted. Touch mid-chest. Flare elbows ~45°, not 90°. Drive bar in a slight J-curve to lockout.
Progressive Overload
When you hit 8 clean reps on all 4 sets, add 2.5 kg next session. If you can't hit 6, reduce load 5%.
🎯 Feel it hereStretch is felt deep in the lower-outer chest (pec minor region) at the bottom of each rep. If you feel front shoulder instead, widen grip slightly.
Incline Dumbbell Press
Dumbbells
3 × 8–12 ▾
Technique
Set bench 30–40°. Dumbbells meet at the top with a slight internal rotation. Lower slowly (3 sec), control the stretch. Don't bounce at the bottom.
Why include
Targets upper chest (clavicular head) and forces independent arm stabilisation. Each side works without the other compensating.
🎯 Feel it hereUpper chest, just below collarbone. Think of spreading your arms wide at the bottom — that tension is the stretch.
Cable Crossover / Pec Deck
Cable / Machine
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
For cables: set pulleys at chest height, slight forward lean, squeeze hands together past midline. Slow eccentric. Constant tension — don't let cables go slack.
Why include
Compounds don't fully adduct the humerus — this fills that gap. Pure chest squeeze at short muscle length builds the inner chest shelf.
🎯 Feel it hereStretch in the outer pec at full extension. Squeeze at centre/peak.
Shoulders — anterior & lateral
Seated Dumbbell Shoulder Press
Dumbbells
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Back supported. Start with dumbbells at ear height. Press overhead without flaring excessively or arching lumbar. Stop just short of lockout to keep tension.
Common fault
Leaning back heavily to make the press easier converts this into an upper chest press, not a shoulder press.
🎯 Feel it hereFront deltoid on press. If you want more side delt, use a lateral raise instead of/after this.
Cable Lateral Raise
Cable
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Single-arm cable from low pulley. Keep slight bend in elbow. Lead with elbow, not wrist. Raise to shoulder height. Lower in 3 sec. Cable maintains tension at bottom unlike dumbbells.
Why cable over dumbbell
Dumbbell lateral raises have near-zero resistance at the bottom. Cable maintains tension throughout the ROM. Better for hypertrophy.
🎯 Feel it hereMedial (side) deltoid. The stretch is felt when arm is low and across body.
⚠ WarningAvoid shrugging the trap to complete the rep — that's trap work, not delt work. If you need to shrug, the weight is too heavy.
Triceps
Overhead Tricep Extension (EZ-bar or Dumbbell)
Barbell / Dumbbell
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Arms overhead, elbows pointing forward and tight. Lower behind head, then extend. Keep upper arms stationary — only forearms move. Overhead position fully stretches the long head.
Why overhead
The tricep long head crosses the shoulder joint. Overhead position puts it in full stretch, maximising hypertrophic stimulus — this is the head that gives tricep mass from behind.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch in the back of the arm / back of armpit when lowered.
⚠ WarningDon't flare elbows wide — this stresses the elbow joint. Keep them pointing straight forward throughout.
Tricep Rope Pushdown
Cable
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Elbows pinned to sides. At the bottom, separate the rope ends and rotate thumbs down to fully contract all three heads. Controlled return — don't let elbows lift.
Why rope over bar
The rope allows wrist pronation at lockout, recruiting more lateral and medial head. Bar keeps wrists neutral and limits this cue.
🎯 Feel it hereSqueeze at the bottom, particularly the horseshoe (lateral head) on the outside of the arm.
Core — anti-extension (push day finisher)
Ab Wheel Rollout
Ab Wheel / Barbell
3 × 8–12 ▾
Technique
Kneel, tuck pelvis under (posterior tilt), and brace hard. Roll out only as far as you can maintain a flat back. Pull back by thinking "ribs to hips". Start with partial range if needed.
Why this over crunches
Anti-extension trains the rectus abdominis the way it actually functions — resisting spinal extension under load. This is what builds visible abs with functional strength underneath.
🎯 Feel it hereEntire anterior core from sternum to pelvis. Deep tension, not just superficial.
⚠ WarningIf your lower back dips or you feel it in your lumbar — stop. Reduce range of motion immediately. This exercise done wrong loads the spine, not the abs.
Plank (weighted progression)
Bodyweight / Plate
3 × 30–60 sec ▾
Technique
Forearms down, elbows under shoulders. Posterior pelvic tilt (tuck tailbone). Squeeze glutes and quads. Don't hold breath — breathe through brace. Add a plate on lower back once 60 sec feels easy.
Progressive overload
Add 5 sec per session until 60 sec, then add a 5 kg plate. This is a real form of progressive overload — don't just "hold it".
🎯 Feel it hereDeep bracing sensation throughout the entire abdominal wall. If you feel nothing — you're not bracing. Squeeze as if about to take a punch.
PULL DAY
Back (lats, rhomboids, traps) · Biceps · Rear Delt · Core (rotation & flexion)

Pull day is your back thickness and width session. Lead with a vertical pull (lat-focused) and a horizontal pull (mid-back focused). Rear delts are critical for shoulder health and the 3D round shoulder look — don't skip them. Biceps come last as they're heavily recruited on all row and pull movements. Core finisher uses rotation patterns.

Lats — back width
Pull-Up / Weighted Pull-Up
Calisthenics / Belt
4 × 5–8 ▾
Technique
Dead hang start. Depress scapulae first (the "shrug down"). Pull elbows to hips — not elbows to ears. Lead chest to the bar. Control the descent over 3 sec. Avoid kipping.
Progression
Can't hit 5? Use lat pulldown first. Can hit 8+ easy? Add a plate via belt. The pull-up is one of the best progressive overload exercises available.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch under the armpit (lat insertion) at the dead hang. Contraction felt along the entire back at top. Not in the biceps.
⚠ WarningDon't skip the dead hang — a partial hang reduces lat recruitment and over-stresses the bicep tendon over time.
Cable Straight-Arm Pulldown
Cable
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Arms straight throughout, slight hinge at hips, pull bar from overhead to thighs by driving elbows down. This is a pure lat isolation — no bicep involvement.
Why include
Most people unconsciously use biceps when doing lat movements. Straight-arm work forces the lat to work in isolation and builds the mind-muscle connection needed for compound movements.
🎯 Feel it hereFull stretch when bar is overhead. Contraction at the thigh when you think "squeeze armpit shut".
Rhomboids / Mid-trap — back thickness
Barbell Bent-Over Row
Barbell
4 × 6–8 ▾
Technique
Hinge until torso is ~45°. Brace core. Pull bar to lower sternum/upper abdomen — not chin. Retract scapulae at top. Don't use momentum or jerk. Overhand or underhand grip both valid.
Overhand vs Underhand
Overhand (pronated) = more upper back, rear delt. Underhand (supinated) = more lat, bicep. Alternate between mesocycles for variety.
🎯 Feel it hereStretch between the shoulder blades at arm extension. Squeeze in the mid/upper back at top — not just the arms pulling.
⚠ WarningKeep lower back neutral throughout. If you're rounding to hit heavier weights — reduce load. Lumbar flexion under a loaded row is a disc injury risk.
Single-Arm Dumbbell Row
Dumbbell
3 × 10–12 (each side) ▾
Technique
Support hand and knee on bench. Let arm hang fully. Pull dumbbell to hip — not armpit. Retract scapula first, then pull. Let the shoulder fully protract at the bottom for full ROM.
Range of motion tip
Allow the shoulder blade to fully drift forward at the bottom — this maximises the stretch. Most people never get the full ROM because they keep the shoulder locked.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch in the lat / under the armpit at full hang. Mid-back squeeze at top.
Rear Deltoid — shoulder health & aesthetics
Face Pull
Cable
3 × 15–20 ▾
Technique
Rope at eye/forehead height. Pull to face, elbows flare above shoulders. At the end position, externally rotate so hands point up (like a double bicep). Squeeze rear delts and rotator cuff.
Why this matters
Push days heavily bias internal rotation and anterior delt. Face pulls counterbalance this, training external rotation. Without this, shoulder impingement creeps in over months of heavy pressing.
🎯 Feel it hereRear/posterior deltoid (back of shoulder cap). Hard to isolate at first — focus on the "show your armpits" external rotation cue at peak contraction.
Biceps
EZ-Bar Bicep Curl
EZ-Bar
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Full hang at bottom — don't cut the rep short. Curl to chin, don't swing. On the eccentric, supinate the wrist (rotate palm up) fully as you lower — this activates more bicep fibres.
EZ vs straight bar
Straight bar more supination = more bicep long head peak. EZ is easier on the wrist and still effective. Alternate between mesocycles.
🎯 Feel it hereFull stretch at the bottom with a slight shoulder flexion. The long head (outer bicep peak) is stretched when elbow is behind the body at the bottom.
Incline Dumbbell Curl
Dumbbells
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Set bench to 45–60°. Arms hang behind the body. This pre-stretches the long head. Curl without bringing elbow forward. The position itself forces honest range — you can't cheat it.
Why include
Stretch-focused curl. The long head of the bicep (peak) is maximally stressed when the arm is behind the torso — a position impossible in standing curls. This is your peak builder.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch in the bicep long head at the bottom. If you feel nothing at the bottom — you're not going low enough.
Core — rotation & flexion (pull day finisher)
Hanging Knee / Leg Raise
Pull-up Bar
3 × 10–15 ▾
Technique
Hang from bar, posterior pelvic tilt before lifting. Raise knees to chest (progression: straight legs). Lower slowly — don't swing. The lowering phase is where most of the work happens.
Progression
Bent knee → straight leg raise → toes-to-bar. Each variation increases resistance and range. Progress when you can do 15 clean reps.
🎯 Feel it hereLower abdominals and hip flexors. The rectus abdominis works hardest as you tuck the pelvis and round the lower back toward the top.
⚠ WarningHip flexor dominance is common here. Focus on rounding the lower back up (posterior pelvic tilt) rather than just lifting the legs — that's the difference between hip flexor work and ab work.
Cable Woodchop / Pallof Press
Cable
3 × 12 each side ▾
Technique (Pallof)
Stand sideways to cable, hold at chest, extend arms straight out. Hold 2 sec. The cable tries to rotate you — you resist. Anti-rotation = oblique and deep core training.
Why anti-rotation
Obliques don't just crunch sideways — they resist and create rotation. Pallof presses and woodchops train the true function of the obliques, which is what gives the "carved" sides of a six-pack.
🎯 Feel it hereExternal obliques on the side facing away from the cable. Subtle but deep — this is low-load, high-stability work.
LEG DAY
Quads · Hamstrings · Glutes · Calves · Core (lateral stability)

Leg day is your biggest hormonal stimulus session — squats and deadlifts release the most growth hormone and testosterone of any exercises. Don't neglect it for upper body aesthetics. Lead with a squat pattern, then a hinge, then isolation. Calves are best trained with full ROM and high rep ranges. Core finisher: lateral stability (obliques, QL).

Quads — primary knee extensors
Barbell Back Squat
Barbell
4 × 5–8 ▾
Technique
Bar on upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Feet shoulder-width, toes 15–30° out. Brace, big breath, break at hip and knee simultaneously. Aim for below parallel. Drive knees out in line with toes on ascent.
Depth note
Below parallel (hip crease below knee) maximises glute and hamstring recruitment. Squat depth is individual — work to your mobility, not a number. Mobility drills on rest days will improve this.
🎯 Feel it hereFull quad stretch at the bottom. Glute and hamstring tension throughout. If you only feel it in your knees — reduce load and work on ankle/hip mobility.
⚠ Warning"Butt wink" (lower back rounding at depth) is a sign of limited hip or ankle mobility. Reduce depth until mobility improves, or elevate heels slightly. Never force depth with a rounded lower back under load.
Leg Press
Machine
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Feet mid-platform, shoulder width. Lower until 90° knee angle minimum — deeper if mobility allows. Don't lock knees at top. Lower foot placement = more quad. Higher = more glute/ham.
Progressive overload
Excellent for accumulating volume safely after heavy squats. The machine allows large load increments — track precisely. Good to go heavier here than you could squat.
🎯 Feel it hereQuad stretch at the bottom near the knee. If your lower back peels off the pad, you've gone too deep for your current hip mobility.
⚠ WarningNever fully lock out the knees at the top under heavy load — this transfers shear force to the knee joint capsule.
Hamstrings & Glutes — posterior chain
Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
Barbell / Dumbbells
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Start standing. Push hips back, keep bar close to legs, maintain neutral spine. Lower until hamstring stretch stops you — usually mid-shin. Drive hips forward to stand. Don't round the lower back. Soft knee bend throughout.
RDL vs Conventional DL
RDL keeps constant tension — you don't rest the bar. It's a stretch-focused movement. Superior for hamstring hypertrophy. Conventional deadlift is more about max strength and full posterior chain.
🎯 Feel it hereThe hamstring stretch behind the knee at the bottom. If you feel it in your lower back instead — you're either loaded too heavy or have insufficient hip hinge pattern. Reduce load and practice the movement unloaded.
⚠ WarningNever round the lower back to extend your range. The range ends where YOUR hamstring flexibility ends. Forcing past that is how disc injuries happen.
Hip Thrust (Barbell or Machine)
Barbell / Machine
3 × 10–15 ▾
Technique
Upper back on bench, bar over hip crease (use pad). Feet flat, pushed slightly away from hips. Drive through heels. At top: ribs down, chin tucked — no lumbar hyperextension. Squeeze glutes for 1 sec at top.
Why include
Squats and RDLs bias the glute at a stretched position. Hip thrust works the glute at peak contraction (shortened). Both positions are needed for full glute development and the round glute aesthetic.
🎯 Feel it hereGlute max at the very top of the movement. If you feel it in your lower back — tuck your chin, push your ribs down, and reduce the range slightly.
Leg Curl (Lying or Seated)
Machine
3 × 10–15 ▾
Seated vs Lying
Seated leg curl keeps the hip in flexion, which puts the hamstring in a longer (more stretched) position. Research suggests superior hypertrophy vs lying curl. If your gym has both, prefer seated.
Technique
Full ROM — let the weight fully extend the knee at the bottom. Curl fully. Slow eccentric (3 sec down). Don't let hips lift off the pad.
🎯 Feel it hereBack of the knee and lower hamstring (biceps femoris) at full extension.
Calves — often undertrained
Standing Calf Raise
Machine / Dumbbell
4 × 12–20 ▾
Technique
Full stretch at the bottom — heel below the platform level. Full contraction at top. 2 sec pause at top. 3 sec eccentric. Calves are extremely resistant to training — they need full ROM and volume to grow.
Common error
Most people use partial reps (bouncing at bottom). This limits ROM and builds nothing. The stretch at the bottom is the most important part of calf training. If it doesn't pull behind your ankle — you're not going deep enough.
🎯 Feel it hereFull stretch in the gastrocnemius (upper calf) and Achilles when heel is fully lowered. Contract the soleus (lower calf) by thinking of pulling your toe up at the top.
Seated Calf Raise
Machine / Dumbbell on knee
3 × 15–20 ▾
Why both
Standing targets gastrocnemius (knee straight). Seated targets soleus (knee bent, which slackens the gastrocnemius). The soleus is a large but often ignored muscle — training it adds real calf thickness and improves ankle stability.
Load note
Soleus responds well to higher reps and slow tempo. Don't rush this — calves need patience.
🎯 Feel it hereDeeper, lower calf (below the gastroc belly). Feels like a different muscle to standing — because it mostly is.
Core — lateral stability (leg day finisher)
Side Plank (weighted progression)
Bodyweight / Plate
3 × 30–45 sec each side ▾
Technique
Elbow under shoulder. Stack feet or stagger. Drive hip up — don't sag. The hip should be higher than you think. Hold. Progress to side plank with hip dip or a plate on hip.
Why lateral work
Squats and deadlifts tax the spine sagittally. Lateral core work trains the quadratus lumborum and obliques — giving the narrow-waist, blocked-abs look and protecting the spine in lateral loading.
🎯 Feel it hereObliques on the top side. QL (deep lower back) on the bottom side. If neither — drive the hip higher.
Dead Bug
Bodyweight / KB
3 × 10 each side ▾
Technique
Lie on back, spine pressed into floor. Arms straight up. Knees 90°. Lower opposite arm and leg simultaneously without letting the lower back arch off the floor. Exhale throughout the movement. Go slow.
Why include
After heavy squats and deadlifts, the dead bug "resets" spinal stability under controlled conditions. It's a rehabilitation staple used to train anti-extension with limb loading — you're getting more from this than it looks.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep transverse abdominis activation throughout. If your back lifts — you've lost the brace. Reset and try again.
REST DAYS
Active Recovery · Cardio · Mobility

Rest days are not wasted days. The question isn't whether to do something — it's what supports recovery without adding stress. Here's what works at your level:

Zone 2 Cardio (20–40 min)

  • Walking, cycling, or light rowing — conversational pace, never breathless
  • Enhances cardiovascular efficiency and nutrient delivery to muscles
  • Does NOT interfere with hypertrophy at this duration and intensity
  • 2 sessions/week maximum. Don't add HIIT on rest days — it delays recovery
  • Best done in the morning before eating, or evening. Either works.

Mobility / Yoga (20–30 min)

  • Target hip flexors, thoracic spine, and ankles — all limit squat/pull ROM
  • Couch stretch: holds hip flexors that get tight from sitting and squatting
  • Cat-cow + thoracic rotation: reverses the rounding from pulling/sitting
  • Ankle circles + calf stretch on step: improves squat depth over 4–8 weeks
  • Yoga flows are excellent here — Yin yoga specifically targets connective tissue

What to Avoid on Rest Days

  • HIIT or intense cardio — adds fatigue without adding muscle
  • Training the same muscles as the day before
  • Long runs (45+ min) at high intensity — cortisol spike limits recovery
  • Complete inactivity: light movement flushes lactic acid better than full rest

Six-Pack: the Real Answer

  • Abs are built in the gym via core finishers (already included)
  • Abs are REVEALED in the kitchen — body fat percentage is the deciding factor
  • Visible abs: ~10–14% body fat for men, ~18–22% for women
  • No amount of crunches will show abs over a layer of fat
  • Cardio on rest days contributes to the caloric deficit that reveals them
// progressive overload — the only rule that matters

Progressive overload means the bar (or the reps, or the difficulty) must go up over time. Without this, your body has no reason to adapt. Here's how to track it simply:

01

Use a notebook or app to log every set, rep, and weight. You cannot "feel" if you're progressing — you must track it. If the number doesn't go up every 1–3 weeks, something is wrong: sleep, food, or programme design.

02

Double progression: aim for the top of your rep range (e.g. 8 reps) on all sets before adding weight. If you hit 4×8 this week, add 2.5 kg next week. If you only hit 4×6, repeat the weight.

03

Deload every 6–8 weeks: drop weight by 40–50% for one full week. This is not weakness — it's how supercompensation works. You come back stronger the week after, not weaker.
