// Training to Failure — The General Rule

Not every set should go to failure — and for progressive overload, most shouldn't. Failure on early sets degrades form and burns neural resources needed for the sets that follow. The goal is proximity to failure, not failure itself.

Use this as your guide: Compound lifts (bench, squat, row, deadlift) — stop 1–2 reps before failure on all sets. Leave reps in reserve. Isolation and machine exercises — the last set can be taken to clean technical failure. Core exercises — never to muscular failure; position quality is the only metric. Each exercise below is tagged: TO FAILURE, RPE 8–9 (1–2 reps left in the tank), or QUALITY REPS (stop at form breakdown, not exhaustion).

PUSH DAY
▸ Warmup ~12 min Workout ~55 min Total ~67 min
Chest · Shoulders · Triceps · Core (anti-extension)

Lead with heavy horizontal pressing when your CNS is fresh. Follow with incline and isolation work as fatigue accumulates. Shoulder isolation comes after chest — your anterior delt is already pre-fatigued from pressing, so you'll need less weight than you expect. Core finisher uses anti-extension patterns: the most functionally accurate way to train the rectus abdominis. Rest 2–3 min between compound sets, 60–90 sec between isolation sets.

Warmup serves three functions: raise core temperature, prime the exact movement patterns you're about to load, and activate specific muscles before they're needed under load. None of these are working sets — use 30–40% of your working weight on bar exercises. Do not rush. A 12-minute warmup prevents months of shoulder injury downtime.

Chest — primary
Barbell Bench Press
RPE 8–9
Barbell
4 × 6–8 ▾
Technique
Retract and depress scapulae before unracking. Slight arch, feet planted. Touch mid-chest. Elbows at ~45°, not flared to 90°. Drive bar in a slight J-curve toward the uprights at lockout.
Progressive Overload
When you hit 8 clean reps on all 4 sets, add 2.5 kg next session. If you miss 6 reps, hold the weight, focus on tempo control, and retry next session before adding load.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch in the lower-outer pec at the bottom of each rep. If you feel the front shoulder instead of the chest — widen grip by one finger width and retest.
⚡ Failure guidanceStop 1–2 reps before failure on sets 1–3. Set 4 can push to RPE 9. Never grind a rep where bar speed drops to near-zero without a reliable spotter and safety catches set.
Incline Dumbbell Press
RPE 8–9
Dumbbells
3 × 8–12 ▾
Technique
Bench at 30–40°. Dumbbells meet at top with slight internal rotation. Lower slowly — 3 sec eccentric, full stretch at bottom. Never bounce off the bottom position.
Why include
Targets the clavicular head of pectoralis major — the line that makes the chest look full from the front. Each arm works independently, eliminating the stronger side compensating for the weaker.
🎯 Feel it hereUpper chest just below the collarbone. Think of spreading your arms wide at the bottom — that stretch across the upper pec is what you're loading.
⚡ Failure guidanceLast set can approach true failure — dumbbells are safer to fail on than a bar. Stop when shoulder position degrades or you can no longer control the eccentric.
Cable Crossover / Pec Deck
To Failure
Cable / Machine
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Cables at chest height, slight forward lean. Squeeze hands together and past the midline. 3-sec eccentric. Constant tension — don't let cables go slack at any point in the rep.
Why include
Pressing movements never fully adduct the humerus. The inner chest "shelf" is built at peak adduction — a position that flat pressing simply cannot achieve. This fills that gap.
🎯 Feel it hereOuter pec stretch at full extension. Inner chest squeeze at the crossover point — hands should pass each other slightly for full adduction.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll 3 sets can go to clean technical failure. No bar overhead — safe to push. Stop when you can no longer complete full adduction, not when arms simply slow down.
Shoulders — anterior & lateral deltoid
Seated Dumbbell Shoulder Press
RPE 8–9
Dumbbells
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Back supported. Dumbbells at ear height. Press overhead without excessive lumbar arch. Stop just short of lockout to keep tension. Keep ribs down throughout.
Common fault
Excessive backward lean converts this into an upper chest press. Torso must stay upright — if you're arching deeply, reduce the load.
🎯 Feel it hereAnterior deltoid (front of shoulder cap) on the press. Peak contraction just before lockout, not at it.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1–2 reps in reserve on all sets. Shoulder pressing to failure risks the dumbbells falling to ear height uncontrolled — a rotator cuff impingement mechanism.
⚠ WarningPinching in the front shoulder at the top means your elbows are too far forward. Move them slightly back so they're directly under the dumbbell at the start position.
Cable Lateral Raise
To Failure
Cable
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Single-arm from low pulley. Slight elbow bend. Lead with the elbow, not the wrist. Raise to shoulder height. 3-sec eccentric. The cable provides resistance at the bottom — unlike a dumbbell which has near-zero load there.
Why cable over dumbbell
Dumbbell lateral raises have almost no resistance in the bottom 30° of the movement. Cable maintains constant tension throughout — more stimulus per rep for the medial delt, which creates shoulder width.
🎯 Feel it hereMedial (side) deltoid — the muscle responsible for shoulder width. Stretch at the lowest point, peak at shoulder height.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Single-arm cable, light load, controlled position — safe to push. Failure = unable to raise elbow to shoulder height without shrugging. That shrug is your stop signal.
⚠ WarningShrugging the trap to complete the rep builds traps, not delts. If you're shrugging, reduce load by 20% and learn the movement before progressing.
Triceps
Overhead Tricep Extension
RPE 8–9
EZ-Bar / Dumbbell
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Arms overhead, elbows pointing straight forward. Lower behind head, then extend. Only forearms move — upper arms are stationary. The overhead position fully stretches the tricep long head, making this categorically different from pushdowns.
Why overhead
The tricep long head crosses the shoulder joint, meaning it only reaches a full stretch in the overhead position. It's the largest head and gives the arm its mass from behind. No pushdown variation achieves this.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep stretch in the back of the arm and into the armpit (long head insertion) at the bottom. If you feel nothing — you're not going low enough or your elbows are flaring outward.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1–2 reps in reserve. Failing overhead with an EZ-bar risks dropping it behind the head. Last set can push to RPE 9 using a single dumbbell held with both hands — safer to control to failure.
⚠ WarningFlaring elbows wide moves stress from the long head to the elbow joint capsule. Elbows point straight forward — not to the sides — for the entire movement. If they flare, reduce load.
Tricep Rope Pushdown
To Failure
Cable — Rope
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Elbows pinned to sides throughout. At full lockout, separate the rope ends and rotate thumbs downward — this fully contracts all three heads. Controlled return; elbows must not drift upward as weight returns.
Rope vs Bar
The rope allows wrist pronation at lockout, recruiting more lateral and medial head fibres simultaneously. A bar keeps wrists neutral and limits this final cue. Rope is the superior choice here.
🎯 Feel it hereThe horseshoe shape on the outer (lateral) tricep head at full lockout — the part visible when the arm hangs at your side.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Standing cable, light-to-moderate load — safe to push fully. Stop when elbows drift from your sides or wrists break, not just when arms slow down.
Core — anti-extension (push day finisher)
Ab Wheel Rollout
Quality Reps
Ab Wheel / Barbell
3 × 8–12 ▾
Technique
Kneel and posteriorly tilt the pelvis (tuck tailbone under) before moving. Brace the entire core hard. Roll out only as far as you maintain a flat lower back. Return by thinking "pull ribs to hips". Start with a short range and add distance week by week.
Why this over crunches
Anti-extension trains the rectus abdominis in its true function: resisting spinal extension under load. This builds visible, functional abs simultaneously. Crunches work a much smaller ROM under far lighter load — less stimulus per rep.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep, sustained tension across the entire anterior core from sternum to pelvis. Not a burn — more like a vice grip across the whole abdominal wall.
⚡ Failure guidanceNever to failure. Stop the moment the lower back dips. One rep with lumbar extension under this lever arm loads the spine dangerously. Shorten your range before you lose position — always.
⚠ WarningIf you feel this in your lower back rather than your abs — stop immediately. Reduce to a 20–30 cm rollout and rebuild from there. This exercise done wrong trains the erectors, not the abs.
Weighted Plank
Quality Reps
Bodyweight → Plate
3 × 30–60 sec ▾
Technique
Forearms flat, elbows under shoulders. Posterior pelvic tilt. Squeeze glutes and quads in addition to abs — this is a full-body tension hold. Breathe through the brace; don't hold your breath. Once 60 sec is easy, add a 5 kg plate on the lower back.
Progressive overload
Add 5 seconds per session until 60 sec is achieved. Then add a 5 kg plate and reset to 30 sec. Track duration and load — this is real progressive overload, not just "holding it".
🎯 Feel it hereDeep bracing tension across the full abdominal wall. If you feel nothing — squeeze harder. The cue: "brace as if you're about to take a punch." That's the correct intensity.
⚡ Failure guidanceStop when hips drop or lower back arches — position failure is the only signal. Discomfort from the hold is fine and expected. Structural breakdown is the stop signal.
PULL DAY
▸ Warmup ~10 min Workout ~55 min Total ~65 min
Back (lats · rhomboids · traps) · Biceps · Rear Delt · Core (rotation)

Pull day is your back width and thickness session. Lead with a vertical pull for lat width, then a horizontal pull for mid-back depth. Rear delts come before biceps — they're critical for shoulder health and the 3D shoulder cap look, and fatigue faster. Biceps come last: they've already been working as secondary movers on every row and pull. Core finisher uses rotation and flexion — the oblique-focused patterns that carve the sides of a six-pack. Rest 2–3 min on compounds, 60–90 sec on isolation.

🔥
Warmup — Pull Day
5 exercises · ~10 min · activate the back before loading it
▾
Pull day warmup focuses on scapular mobility, lat activation, and elbow/bicep tendon preparation. Most back injuries occur when pulling under load before the rotator cuff and scapular stabilisers are primed. These 10 minutes directly prevent the most common pulling injuries: bicep tendon strain and shoulder impingement.

Lats — back width
Pull-Up / Weighted Pull-Up
RPE 8–9
Bodyweight / Belt
4 × 5–8 ▾
Technique
Start from a dead hang. Depress scapulae first ("shrug downward" before bending the elbows). Pull elbows toward the hips — not the ears. Lead with the chest to the bar. 3-sec controlled descent. No kipping.
Progression
Can't hit 5 reps? Use lat pulldown until strength builds. Hitting 8+ easily? Add load via a dip belt. The weighted pull-up is one of the best progressive overload vehicles in existence.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep lat stretch under the armpit at dead hang. Contraction along the entire back from armpit to lower back at the top. If you only feel biceps — the scapular depression cue is missing.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1–2 reps in reserve. Failing a pull-up means uncontrolled drop from height — bicep tendon or shoulder strain risk. Stop when chin no longer clears the bar with controlled form.
⚠ WarningNever skip the dead hang start. Partial hangs (slight elbow bend at bottom) reduce lat recruitment and chronically stress the bicep tendon insertion at the elbow over time.
Cable Straight-Arm Pulldown
To Failure
Cable
3 × 12–15 ▾
Technique
Arms straight throughout — zero elbow bend. Slight hip hinge. Pull bar from overhead to thighs in an arc by driving elbows down and through. Pure lat isolation with no bicep contribution.
Why include
Most people unconsciously recruit their biceps in lat movements. Straight-arm work eliminates bicep contribution entirely and builds the mind-muscle connection needed to get more from every compound pulling movement.
🎯 Feel it hereFull lat stretch at the overhead position. Deep contraction along the side of the back at the hip — think "squeeze your armpit shut".
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Standing cable, no bar overhead — safe to push. Stop when elbows begin to bend (bicep compensating), not just when arms slow down.
Rhomboids / Mid-trap — back thickness
Barbell Bent-Over Row
RPE 8–9
Barbell
4 × 6–8 ▾
Technique
Hinge until torso is ~45°. Brace core hard before each rep. Pull bar to lower sternum/upper abdomen — not the chin. Fully retract scapulae at the top. No jerking or momentum. Overhand or underhand grip both valid — alternate between training blocks.
Grip comparison
Overhand (pronated): more upper back, rear delt, rhomboid. Underhand (supinated): more lat and bicep. Alternate between mesocycles to develop the full back and prevent pattern overuse.
🎯 Feel it hereStretch between the shoulder blades at full arm extension. Mid-upper back squeeze at the top — if you only feel arms pulling, you're not retracting the scapulae.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1–2 reps in reserve always. Failure in a bent-over position under heavy load causes lumbar rounding — the primary mechanism for disc injury on this exercise. Stop when bar speed drops significantly or your lower back brace wavers.
⚠ WarningLower back rounding to handle heavier load is the most common dangerous fault here. The muscles being targeted are in the upper back — load that your lower back can't support isn't training your target muscles, it's just risk.
Single-Arm Dumbbell Row
RPE 8–9
Dumbbell
3 × 10–12 each side ▾
Technique
Support hand and knee on bench. Let the working arm hang fully — allow the shoulder to fully protract at the bottom. Retract the scapula first, then bend the elbow. Pull to the hip, not the armpit. The shoulder blade should visibly travel from protracted to retracted.
ROM tip
Most people never allow full shoulder protraction at the bottom, cutting the ROM in half. That lost half contains the entire stretch phase — the phase most responsible for hypertrophic stimulus. Let the shoulder reach forward every rep.
🎯 Feel it hereLat and mid-back stretch at the full hang. Scapular retraction and mid-back squeeze at the top — not just a bicep curl.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1 rep in reserve. Failure on a dumbbell row typically results in torso rotation to compensate — this shifts load to the lower back rather than the target muscles.
Rear Deltoid — shoulder health & aesthetics
Face Pull
To Failure
Cable — Rope
3 × 15–20 ▾
Technique
Rope at eye/forehead height. Pull to the face with elbows flaring above shoulder height. At end position, externally rotate so hands finish pointing upward (like a double bicep pose). Hold 1 sec. Two phases: pull and rotate. Most people only do the pull.
Why this matters
Push days heavily bias internal rotation and the anterior delt. Without external rotation work, the shoulder capsule tightens anteriorly over months — leading to impingement. Face pulls are one of the most important shoulder health exercises that most people consistently skip.
🎯 Feel it herePosterior deltoid (back of the shoulder cap) and external rotators. Hard to isolate initially — focus on the "show your armpits" external rotation cue at peak contraction.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Light load, safe position. Stop when you can no longer achieve full external rotation at the end position (hands pointing up). That's technical failure.
Biceps
EZ-Bar Bicep Curl
RPE 8–9
EZ-Bar
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Full hang at the bottom — never cut the ROM short. Curl to chin without body swing. On the eccentric, fully supinate the wrist as you lower — this recruits more bicep fibres through the complete range.
EZ-Bar vs Straight Bar
Straight bar forces more supination = more long head recruitment (outer peak). EZ-bar is easier on the wrists and still highly effective. Alternate between blocks.
🎯 Feel it hereLong head (outer bicep) stretch at the bottom. If you allow a slight shoulder extension at the bottom (elbow slightly behind the hip), the long head stretch is even more pronounced.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1 rep in reserve. Bicep failure often triggers a momentum jerk to complete the rep — this is a bicep tendon injury mechanism at the elbow. Stop before you swing.
Incline Dumbbell Curl
To Failure
Dumbbells
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Bench at 45–60°. Arms hang behind the body at the bottom — this pre-stretches the long head before each rep. Curl without bringing the elbow forward. The position makes cheating structurally impossible and forces honest ROM.
Why include
The bicep long head (peak) is maximally stressed when the arm is behind the torso — a position standing curls cannot achieve. Use lighter weight than expected. This is your peak builder.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep long head stretch at the bottom — in the front of the upper arm/front of shoulder. If you feel nothing at the bottom, the bench is too upright or you're not allowing the arm to hang fully behind you.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. The inclined position eliminates cheating — when you genuinely fail, the dumbbell simply won't move without the elbow coming forward, which is a clear and safe natural stopping signal.
Core — flexion & rotation (pull day finisher)
Hanging Knee / Leg Raise
Quality Reps
Pull-Up Bar
3 × 10–15 ▾
Technique
Hang from bar. Posteriorly tilt pelvis (tuck tailbone) before lifting anything. Raise knees to chest or straight legs to 90°+. Lower in a controlled 3-sec eccentric. No swinging. The lowering phase carries most of the stimulus.
Progression
Bent knee → straight leg raise → toes-to-bar. Advance when you can do 15 clean, slow reps. Never advance by adding momentum — that's not progression, it's just hip flexors.
🎯 Feel it hereLower abdominals at the bottom of the eccentric. Rectus abdominis peak at the top when the pelvis posteriorly tilts and the lower back rounds slightly as knees reach the chest.
⚡ Failure guidanceStop when you can no longer control the eccentric or when hips begin to swing. Momentum means hip flexors are doing the work — not the abs. Quality reps only.
⚠ WarningHip flexor dominance is the most common fault. If you feel it entirely in the hip crease — the posterior pelvic tilt is missing. Tuck the tailbone before every rep, every time.
Pallof Press
Quality Reps
Cable
3 × 12 each side ▾
Technique
Stand sideways to cable, cable at chest height. Hold with both hands at your sternum. Press arms straight out in front — hold 2 sec — return. The cable pulls you to rotate toward it. Your entire job is to resist that rotation completely. Hips and shoulders stay perfectly square forward.
Why anti-rotation
Obliques don't just create rotation — their primary function is to resist and control it. This is what creates the carved side definition of a six-pack. Crunches don't train obliques in this anti-rotation pattern. Pallof press does.
🎯 Feel it hereExternal oblique on the side facing away from the cable. Subtle but deep. If you feel nothing — increase load slightly or slow the press to a 3-sec extension.
⚡ Failure guidanceStop when hips or shoulders begin to rotate toward the cable. Never to muscular failure — form failure is the only measure that counts here.
LEG DAY
▸ Warmup ~15 min Workout ~60 min Total ~75 min
Quads · Hamstrings · Glutes · Calves · Core (lateral)

Leg day is your highest hormonal stimulus session — squats and deadlifts trigger more systemic growth hormone and testosterone release than any other exercise. The warmup here is the longest of the three because the hip, knee, and ankle require substantially more preparation than the shoulder joint. Lead with squat (knee-dominant), follow with RDL (hip-dominant), then isolation. Calves need full ROM and genuine volume — undertrained calves are one of the most common physique bottlenecks. Core finisher: lateral stability.

🔥
Warmup — Leg Day
7 exercises · ~15 min · hips, ankles, and glutes all need separate activation
▾
Leg day warmup is the most critical of the three. Cold hip flexors, restricted ankles, and inactive glutes under a loaded squat is the standard recipe for knee cave, butt wink, and lower back injury. Your squat depth and safety depend directly on the quality of these 15 minutes. Never skip or shorten this warmup on leg day.

Ramp-Up Sets — Squat
~50% × 5 reps, then ~70% × 3 reps. These should feel easy — neural priming only, not tiring.
2 ramp sets
~3 min
Progressively loads the movement before working sets. Identifies any sharp knee or hip discomfort at sub-maximal loads — a clear signal to reduce load today.
Quads — primary knee extensors
Barbell Back Squat
RPE 8–9
Barbell
4 × 5–8 ▾
Technique
Bar on upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Feet shoulder-width, toes 15–30° out. Brace hard, hold your breath (Valsalva). Break at hip and knee simultaneously. Drive knees out over toes on descent and ascent. Aim for at least parallel.
High bar vs low bar
High bar: more upright torso, more quad emphasis, allows deeper depth for most people. Low bar: more hip-dominant, mechanically stronger, more hamstring/glute. For aesthetics, high bar is typically preferred.
🎯 Feel it hereFull quad stretch at the bottom. Glute and hamstring tension throughout the range. If you only feel it in the knees — reduce weight and prioritise the ankle/hip warmup drills consistently over the next 4–6 weeks.
⚡ Failure guidanceNever to failure without a spotter and safety bars set. Leave 2 reps in reserve on all sets. A failed squat without safety equipment is extremely dangerous. Always set the safeties before your first set.
⚠ Warning"Butt wink" (lower back rounding at depth) is a mobility limitation, not a technique error you can simply fix by trying harder. Reduce depth to where your lower back stays neutral and work on the warmup drills. Never force depth with spinal rounding under a loaded bar.
Leg Press
RPE 8–9
Machine
3 × 10–12 ▾
Technique
Feet mid-platform, shoulder-width. Lower until at least 90° knee angle — deeper if lower back stays flat on the pad. Never lock out knees at the top under load. Lower foot placement = more quad emphasis. Higher = more glute/hamstring.
Why after squats
Excellent for safe volume accumulation after the CNS-demanding squat. Machine support allows higher loads than free squatting. Track weights precisely — this machine is ideal for progressive overload logging.
🎯 Feel it hereQuad stretch near the knee at the bottom. If the lower back peels from the pad, you've exceeded your current hip mobility — reduce depth.
⚡ Failure guidanceLast set can push to near-failure. Stop short of full knee lockout at the top regardless of fatigue level — locking out under heavy leg press load stresses the knee capsule.
⚠ WarningNever fully lock the knees at the top under heavy load. Stop 5–10° short of full extension every rep.
Hamstrings & Glutes — posterior chain
Romanian Deadlift (RDL)
RPE 8–9
Barbell / Dumbbells
3 × 8–10 ▾
Technique
Start standing. Push hips backward — not downward. Keep bar dragging close to the legs. Neutral spine throughout. Lower until the hamstring stretch stops you — typically mid-shin. Drive hips forward to return to standing. Soft knee bend throughout. Bar never touches the floor.
RDL vs Conventional DL
RDL maintains constant tension — no floor rest. It's stretch-focused, maximising hamstring hypertrophy stimulus. Conventional deadlift is max-strength focused from a dead stop. For aesthetics and posterior chain development, RDL provides the more direct stimulus.
🎯 Feel it hereStrong hamstring pull behind the knee and into the glute at the lowest point. If you feel the lower back instead — you're either overloaded or rounding the spine. This is a hip hinge, not a back movement.
⚡ Failure guidanceLeave 1–2 reps in reserve always. Lower back rounding under a loaded hip hinge is the classic disc injury mechanism. Form failure is the absolute stop signal regardless of rep count.
⚠ WarningYour range ends where your hamstring flexibility currently ends — not further. Forcing past that with spinal flexion is how lumbar disc issues accumulate silently over weeks before becoming injuries.
Hip Thrust
RPE 8–9
Barbell / Machine
3 × 10–15 ▾
Technique
Upper back on bench edge, bar across hip crease (use a pad). Feet flat and pushed slightly forward. Drive through heels. At the top: chin tucked, ribs down — no lumbar hyperextension. Squeeze glutes hard for 1 full second at the top every rep.
Why include alongside squats
Squats and RDLs work the glute most effectively in the stretched position. Hip thrust works the glute at peak contraction (shortened position). Training both positions is required for complete glute development — you cannot get the full glute from squatting alone.
🎯 Feel it hereGlute maximus hard contraction at the top — not the hamstring, not the lower back. If you feel the lower back, your ribs are flaring and you're hyperextending the lumbar instead of extending the hip. Tuck the chin and push the ribs down.
⚡ Failure guidanceLast 1–2 sets can push to near-failure. Stop when the lower back begins taking over from the glutes — that's the technical failure point regardless of rep count.
Leg Curl (Seated preferred)
To Failure
Machine
3 × 10–15 ▾
Seated vs Lying
Seated keeps the hip in flexion, placing the hamstring in a longer starting position — the position from which stretch-mediated hypertrophy is maximised. Research consistently favours seated over lying for hamstring growth. Use seated if your gym has it.
Technique
Full ROM — complete knee extension at the bottom. Full curl at the top. 3-sec eccentric. Don't let hips lift off the pad (lying) or lean forward excessively (seated) to compensate for heavy load.
🎯 Feel it hereHamstring stretch at full knee extension. Biceps femoris (outer/lower hamstring) most prominent. Full hamstring contraction at peak curl.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Machine isolation, fixed axis — safe to push. Stop when hips lift (lying) or you lose control of the eccentric.
Calves — most consistently undertrained muscle group
Standing Calf Raise
To Failure
Machine / Dumbbell
4 × 12–20 ▾
Technique
Heel fully below platform level at bottom. Rise to full plantarflexion at top. 2-sec hold at the top. 3-sec eccentric. Calves are fatigue-resistant by design — they walk with you every day. They need full ROM, volume, and progressive overload to respond to training.
Most common error
Partial reps — bouncing at the bottom and never reaching full heel drop. This trains the calf in its mid-range only. The stretch at full heel drop is the most hypertrophically valuable part of the movement. If it doesn't pull behind your ankle — you're not going low enough.
🎯 Feel it hereGastrocnemius (upper calf belly) and Achilles tendon at full heel drop. Think of "pushing the floor away with your heel" at the top to maximise the peak contraction.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Calves are highly fatigue-resistant and safe to push. Stop when you can no longer achieve the full heel-to-peak ROM — not just when it burns.
Seated Calf Raise
To Failure
Machine / Dumbbell on knee
3 × 15–20 ▾
Why both
Standing targets the gastrocnemius (knee straight). Seated targets the soleus (knee bent slackens the gastrocnemius, forcing the soleus to work). The soleus is a large, deep muscle most people never directly train — it adds real calf depth and thickness that the gastrocnemius alone cannot provide.
Technique
Same full ROM as standing — heel fully dropped, full rise. Soleus responds well to slow tempo and moderate-high reps. Don't rush this exercise; the soleus is a slow-twitch dominant muscle.
🎯 Feel it hereDeeper, lower calf below the gastrocnemius belly. Genuinely feels like a different muscle — because it largely is.
⚡ Failure guidanceAll sets to failure. Same principle as standing — ROM collapse is the signal, not just burning.
Core — lateral stability (leg day finisher)
Side Plank (weighted progression)
Quality Reps
Bodyweight → Plate
3 × 30–45 sec each side ▾
Technique
Elbow directly under shoulder. Stack feet or stagger. Drive hip up — higher than feels natural. Hold. Progress to hip dip variation or add a plate on the hip once 45 sec is comfortable.
Why lateral work
Squats and deadlifts tax the spine in the sagittal plane only. Lateral core work (obliques, quadratus lumborum) protects the spine against lateral loads and builds the narrow-waist, segmented look that makes abs visible from the front.
🎯 Feel it hereExternal oblique on the top side. QL (deep lower back) on the bottom side. If you feel neither — drive the hip higher and squeeze harder.
⚡ Failure guidanceStop when the hip sags toward the floor. Position-based failure, not time-based.
Dead Bug
Quality Reps
Bodyweight / Kettlebell
3 × 10 each side ▾
Technique
Lie on back. Press lower back into the floor — keep it there throughout. Arms up, knees 90°. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg while preventing the lower back from lifting. Exhale fully throughout each rep. Full reset between reps.
Why post leg-day
After heavy compound leg work, dead bug resets spinal stability in a deloaded position. It trains anti-extension with limb loading — the same pattern as the ab wheel but controlled and from the floor. You're getting more from this than it appears.
🎯 Feel it hereDeep transverse abdominis across the lower abdominal wall. Subtle but genuine. If the back lifts from the floor — that rep doesn't count. Reset.
⚡ Failure guidanceOne rep with the lower back lifting is worse than stopping two reps short. Stop at the first sign of back lift — not at muscular exhaustion.
REST DAYS
Active Recovery · Zone 2 · Mobility

Rest days are where adaptation happens — your body repairs and grows during recovery, not during the session itself. The question is never "should I do something?" but "what supports recovery without adding stress?"

Zone 2 Cardio (20–40 min)

  • Walking, cycling, or light rowing — conversational pace throughout, never breathless
  • Enhances mitochondrial density and nutrient delivery to recovering muscle tissue
  • Does not interfere with hypertrophy at this duration and intensity level
  • Maximum 2 sessions/week. HIIT on rest days delays muscle recovery, not accelerates it
  • Morning or evening — both work equally well for recovery purposes

Mobility / Yoga (20–30 min)

  • Target: hip flexors, thoracic spine, and ankles — all directly limit compound lift ROM
  • Couch stretch (60 sec/side): releases hip flexors shortened by sitting and squatting
  • Cat-cow + thoracic rotation: reverses spinal rounding accumulated from pulling
  • Ankle dorsiflexion drill: the single most impactful squat depth improver available
  • Yin yoga (held 3–5 min): targets connective tissue and fascia — different from vinyasa

What to Avoid

  • HIIT or high-intensity cardio — adds fatigue without contributing to hypertrophy
  • Training the same muscle group worked in the preceding 24 hours
  • Sustained running above 40 min at high intensity — elevated cortisol suppresses recovery
  • Complete inactivity: a 20-min walk actively flushes lactate better than lying still

The Six-Pack Answer

  • Abs are built through the core finishers embedded in every session
  • Abs are revealed by reducing body fat — diet is the only variable that changes visibility
  • Visible abs typically appear at ~10–14% BF (men) or ~18–22% BF (women)
  • No rep volume of crunches removes the fat layer sitting above the abdominal muscles
  • Zone 2 cardio on rest days creates the caloric deficit that reveals what you're already building
// progressive overload — the only rule that matters

Your body has no reason to add muscle unless it's consistently asked to do more than before. Progressive overload is the mechanism that forces adaptation. Everything else — nutrition, sleep, technique — exists to serve this principle.

01

Track every set, rep, and weight without exception. You cannot "feel" progress — you must see it written. If a number hasn't moved in 3 weeks: sleep, calories, or programme design is the problem. Investigate in that order.

02

Double progression: Hit the top of your rep range (e.g., 8) on every set before adding load. Hit 4×8 this week → add 2.5 kg next week. Stuck at 4×6 → repeat the weight, fix the technique or rest quality first.

03

Deload every 6–8 weeks: Drop to 50% load for one full week. This is how supercompensation works. The week after a proper deload is consistently the strongest week of any training block, not the weakest.
